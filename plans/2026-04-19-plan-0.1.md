# Friski (frisco.wiki) — Project Plan

**Version**: Draft 0.1 — April 2026
**Maintainer**: Luis Villa

## 1. Project Overview

### 1.1 What Friski is

Friski is an experimental civic wiki covering San Francisco topics at hyper-local granularity — neighborhoods (Calle 24), local politicians, civic institutions, ongoing disputes, small landmarks. Content is synthesized primarily from reputable local news sources (Mission Local, SF Standard, SF Public Press, SF Chronicle, 48 Hills, etc.) with systematic attention to source discipline.

Friski is equally a research lab testing what a post-LLM wiki can look like: git-backed content, atomic commits, LLM-assisted contributions with human merge authority, and LLM-backed CI that enforces editorial discipline as executable policy.

### 1.2 Research thesis

The hypothesis under test:

> LLM-backed CI can enforce editorial discipline (source verification, claim-prose coherence, NPOV) well enough that a novel workflow produces content a thoughtful reader would trust and find useful.

Workflow and content are bound together in this evaluation. A workflow that produces untrustworthy or useless content has failed, even if the machinery runs. Conversely, good content produced by hand around the workflow doesn’t validate the workflow. The test is whether the workflow, operating as designed, produces output that passes a competent reader’s smell test — accurate, well-sourced, appropriately attributed, free of the characteristic failure modes of LLM-assisted content.

This framing shapes MVP scope. The goal is not breadth (a comprehensive SF civic resource) but sufficient depth on a bounded set of articles to demonstrate the workflow works end-to-end on content that actually stands up. An article that passes the smell test, produced via the full workflow machinery including reviewer checks, is worth more as evidence than ten articles produced by hand. Friski’s content is both the demonstration and a real artifact; it doesn’t need to become a site of active neighborhood contestation at MVP scale, but it does need to be content a reader could cite without embarrassment.

If the hypothesis holds, the patterns generalize beyond a single civic wiki and content breadth becomes a later-phase question. If it doesn’t, we’ll have learned something useful about where LLM-assisted editorial workflows break down.

### 1.3 What Friski is explicitly *not*

- Not a replacement for Wikipedia or MediaWiki.
- Not trying to be a complete SF encyclopedia at MVP scale (at least not yet!)
- Not a general-purpose AI writing tool.
- Not a community-driven wiki in the Wikipedia sense — at MVP, governance is held by a small team, with carefully scoped contributor entry points.

### 1.4 Umbrella

Friski will borrow from the `open-graph-next`/`wikidata-SIFT` experiment, sharing methodology (SIFT verification), infrastructure (LLM reviewer harness), and narrative (what comes after MediaWiki/Wikidata). But it will live in a separate set of GitHub repositories.

## 2. Editorial Principles

These principles are load-bearing. They shape the LLM reviewer prompts, the bot’s behavior, and the governance model. This section should be kept current and treated as the normative reference. Disagreements about whether a given edit is acceptable should be resolvable by pointing at this section.

### 2.1 Neighborhood NPOV

Traditional Wikipedia NPOV assumes a large pool of reliable secondary sources and a global audience. Neither condition holds at the neighborhood level. Friski’s NPOV principles are adapted for this reality:

**Attribute early and often.** Where Wikipedia might say “the development is controversial,” Friski says “Mission Local reported in 2024 that the development drew opposition from Calle 24 Latino Cultural District advocates, who argued X; the developer’s representative responded Y (Chronicle, 2024).” Attribution shifts the epistemic frame from “here is the truth” to “here is what specific sources reported,” which is more defensible at local scale and more honest about the limits of what can be known.

**Name the disputants, not the dispute.** Avoid framing like “critics argue” or “supporters claim” without naming who. For local disputes, the specific actors matter — residents of a particular block, a specific merchants’ association, a named advocacy group. Naming is more accurate and less manipulable.

**Represent multiple framings when they exist.** If Mission Local and the Standard cover the same dispute and reach different conclusions, represent both framings with attribution. Don’t synthesize an artificial consensus. If one outlet is demonstrably more careful on a given topic, the reviewer and editors may weight accordingly, but the divergence itself is part of the record.

**Distinguish reporting from opinion.** Editorials, op-eds, and columnists’ takes are acceptable sources for attributed opinion claims, not for factual claims. “The Chronicle editorial board endorsed X” is a factual claim about an editorial stance. “X is the right policy” quoted from an editorial is an opinion claim and must be attributed as such.

**Acknowledge uncertainty.** When sources disagree about simple facts (vote counts, dates, attendance figures), say so. “Sources differ on the final vote count (Mission Local reported 8-3; SF Standard reported 7-4).” This is honest and stable against future correction.

**Be cautious with living people.** Biographical claims about living people need stronger sourcing than claims about institutions or events. This is standard BLP discipline and applies fully to Friski. For living local politicians, activists, and public figures, claims must be sourced to named reporting, not to blog posts, social media, or unnamed sources, except in specific cases (see §2.2).

**No advocacy voice.** Friski articles do not argue for policies or outcomes. An article can document that Calle 24 advocates argued for specific preservation policies; it does not itself argue that those policies are correct. This is tested in reviewer prompts by checking for first-person or exhortative language in the prose.

### 2.2 Reliable sources, adapted for local scale

The traditional “reliable secondary sources” standard breaks down below a certain granularity. Much of what’s worth saying about Calle 24 isn’t covered in secondary sources, because the scale is too small for secondary coverage to exist. Friski’s source standards are adapted accordingly.

**Tier 1 — Primary reliable.** Usable for most factual claims without further corroboration.

- Professional local journalism with bylined reporting and visible editorial practices: Mission Local, SF Public Press, SF Standard, SF Chronicle, KQED, 48 Hills (with awareness of editorial stance).
- Regional professional journalism: East Bay Express, Bay City News, Bay Area Reporter (for LGBTQ+ coverage), El Tecolote (for Mission/Latino coverage).
- Academic publications and university-affiliated research.
- Court filings and judicial opinions (citations to PACER, CourtListener).
- Peer-reviewed research.

**Tier 2 — Primary sources requiring context.** Acceptable for factual claims where the source is best positioned to know and doesn’t have obvious bias on the specific claim. Use with attribution.

- Government records: SF Planning Department filings, Board of Supervisors legislation and meeting minutes, budget documents, campaign finance filings. These are primary sources of fact for what a government body did, with attribution to the specific document.
- Organizational websites for non-controversial facts about the organization itself: “Calle 24 Latino Cultural District was formally designated in 2014” cited to the organization’s own site is acceptable; “Calle 24 is the most important cultural district in San Francisco” cited to the same source is not.
- Academic or public lectures, panel discussions (with clear attribution to the speaker).
- Transcripts of public meetings (including YouTube recordings of Board of Supervisors sessions, archived at their official sources).

**Tier 3 — Sources requiring heightened scrutiny.** Usable with care, ideally corroborated by Tier 1/2.

- Personal blogs of named journalists or researchers with relevant expertise.
- Substack and similar independent publications with clear bylines and editorial practices.
- Neighborhood association newsletters.
- SF Weekly, SFist, Hoodline — variable editorial quality over their histories, check the era of the specific article.

**Tier 4 — Use only in specific circumstances.**

- Social media posts from verified public figures, only as primary evidence of *what that person said* (never as evidence of external facts).
- Archived versions of defunct publications, with awareness of original editorial quality.
- Community-sourced platforms (Nextdoor, reddit r/sanfrancisco) — generally not acceptable as sources; occasionally usable as evidence of public discourse with very heavy attribution (“in a widely shared Nextdoor post, a resident claimed…”) and never for factual claims about third parties.

**Not acceptable as sources:**

- Anonymous blog posts.
- PR releases and advocacy materials, except as evidence of what the releasing organization said.
- AI-generated content from other sources.
- Circular citations (Wikipedia citing Friski citing Wikipedia).

**Source durability.** Every citation should include an archive.org link at the time of claim creation. Link rot is a known problem for civic content; archiving at citation time should be non-negotiable and enforced in CI, but given the increased rise of paywalls this may have to be finessed.

**Conflict of interest.** There is no conflict-of-interest policy yet. In theory, a combination of clear requirements for claims and sourcing rules should mitigate this problem, but we’ll learn more as the experiment develops.

### 2.3 Scope discipline

Friski is explicitly hyperlocal. At MVP:

- Scope is limited to San Francisco proper, with occasional necessary Bay Area context.
- Neighborhoods, institutions, events, and people of genuine civic relevance are in scope.
- Individual residents, private businesses without civic significance, and purely personal disputes are not in scope.
- Living private individuals are not subjects of articles. Public figures acting in public roles are in scope for their public roles.
- Scope may expand in later phases. MVP does not try to be exhaustive.

### 2.4 What “releasable” looks like for an article

An article is releasable when:

1. Every factual claim has a source at Tier 1-3 (or Tier 4 with explicit justification).
1. Every source has an archive link.
1. The claims block in frontmatter is consistent with the prose (LLM reviewer check).
1. BLP claims about living people are at Tier 1-2.
1. Multiple framings are represented for any topic where sources disagree.
1. No advocacy voice.
1. Article is tagged with scope (neighborhood, person, institution, event, etc.) for indexing.

Note that releasable is *not* “done”; a stub may be releasable under these criteria and of course articles about ongoing events are never “done”.

To be determined: to what extent starting with stubs is a healthy practice, in a way that might have worked for early English Wikipedia but would get an article immediately deleted now.

## 3. Technical Architecture

### 3.1 Stack

**Forge**: GitHub at MVP. Mirror to Codeberg after first successful CI tests to test portability and surface the AI-skepticism question. Canonical home may migrate later; git repo portability makes this a cheap decision.

**Static site generator**: TBD. Initial suggestion is Astro for the islands architecture (selective interactivity for the governance surface), Markdown-first authoring, active ecosystem, and fit with mixed static + light-interactive pages.

**Hosting**: Netlify for the rendered site. `frisco.wiki` DNS points here.

**Content format**: Exact blend between prose Markdown, structured YAML, and SQLite TBD. First pass proposal, will refine before implementation: Markdown with YAML frontmatter. Prose is the primary artifact; frontmatter carries structured claims. Two-way consistency between prose and frontmatter is an LLM reviewer responsibility, not a structural property of the format. Claim database is SQLite, derived at build time from article frontmatter. Stored as a simple triple-ish schema (see §3.3). Not an authoritative store — the Markdown is authoritative; SQLite is a build artifact for querying.

**CI**: Self-hosted runner for LLM access. Runs on a small VPS (Hetzner, DigitalOcean, or similar—already have a fly.dev account). Receives webhooks from GitHub (and eventually Codeberg). CI logic is written as plain shell scripts invoked by thin YAML wrappers, to preserve portability across forges.

**LLM reviewer**: Adapted from the `wikidata-SIFT` experiment’s methodology. Ideally multi-reviewer and cheap, but can start with opus or sonnet. Reviewer outputs structured YAML logs mirroring the existing experiment’s log format.

**Bot**: Claude Code operating against the repo, opening atomic PRs. Never merges. Deferred past MVP Phase 0-1.

**Merge model**: Fast-forward patch series. No merge commits, no squash. Each commit on `main` is an atomic unit that can be reverted cleanly.

### 3.2 Portability discipline

The following patterns are load-bearing because Codeberg migration is planned:

- CI logic in shell scripts (`scripts/check-*.sh`), invoked by thin forge-specific wrappers.
- Bot forge operations behind a minimal interface (`open_pr`, `comment`, `label`) with GitHub implementation now, Forgejo implementation later.
- Structured metadata lives in commit messages and PR bodies (YAML blocks), not in forge-native fields like labels or projects.
- Static site hosting is separate from the git forge (Cloudflare Pages, not GitHub Pages).
- No use of GitHub-specific features: Projects, Discussions, Codespaces, Copilot PR features.

A weekly mirroring check surfaces portability regressions: if the Codeberg mirror diverges, investigate.

### 3.3 Claim data model

To be confirmed: Claims are represented in article frontmatter. At build time, they’re extracted into SQLite for querying.

**Schema** (illustrative; actual DDL in `schema/claims.sql`):

```
claims: (claim_id, article_slug, subject_id, property_id, value, start_date, end_date, source_id, confidence, coi, line_number)
sources: (source_id, publication, url, archive_url, archive_date, author, date_published, tier, notes)
subjects: (subject_id, label, wikidata_qid, type, description)
```

**Property/subject identifiers**:

- Reuse Wikidata Q-numbers and P-numbers where applicable (Scott Wiener = Q7437504, position held = P39, start time = P580).
- For Friski-local entities and properties without Wikidata equivalents, use a clear prefix (TBD, but eg `F-`). Never collides with Wikidata IDs. Enables future federation.
- Sources are first-class entities, with structure borrowed from WikiCite.

**Example article frontmatter**:

```yaml
---
title: Connie Chan
slug: connie-chan
type: person
subject_id: Q107616036
scope: [person, politician]
tags: [district-1, board-of-supervisors]
claims:
  - id: C001
    property: P39  # position held
    value: F-SF-D1-Supervisor
    start: 2021-01-08
    end: null
    source: sfchron-2021-01-08-chan-swearing-in
  - id: C002
    property: P108  # employer
    value: Q1128418  # SF Board of Supervisors
    start: 2021-01-08
    end: null
    source: sfchron-2021-01-08-chan-swearing-in
---

Connie Chan is an American politician serving as a member of the
San Francisco Board of Supervisors, representing District 1
since January 2021 [C001].
```

**Claim references in prose**: TBD exact format, but something like: Optional `[C001]` markers in prose link prose assertions to claim IDs. This is a soft convention, not required for MVP; it helps the LLM reviewer check coherence and may become a rendered link in later phases.

### 3.4 LLM reviewer

The reviewer is the most novel technical element and the primary locus of research contribution. It runs on every PR and emits structured findings.

**Ported from `wikidata-SIFT`**: SIFT methodology (Stop, Investigate source, Find better coverage, Trace to original context), confidence scoring, YAML provenance logs.

**Friski-specific checks**:

1. **Claim-prose coherence**: For each factual assertion in prose, is it backed by a claim? (The reverse need not be true; some claims will be worth tracking but not worth surfacing in the prose.)
1. **Source support**: Fetch the cited source. Does it actually support the specific claim being made?
1. **Source tier compliance**: Is the source at an acceptable tier for the claim type? (BLP claims need Tier 1-2; etc.)
1. **Archive presence**: Does every source have an archive link?
1. **NPOV flags**: Does the prose use loaded language, advocacy voice, or unattributed editorial claims?
1. **Cross-article consistency**: Does this claim contradict a claim in another article?
1. **Duplicate detection**: Is this claim redundant with an existing claim in another article?
1. **Scope check**: Is the article subject in scope per §2.3?

**Operational properties**:

- Temperature 0, version-pinned model, cached responses for unchanged inputs. Determinism is a design goal even if not fully achievable.
- Structured output (YAML) parseable by tooling.
- Findings posted as PR review comments with specific line references.
- Human override is a first-class path: maintainers can merge despite reviewer objections with a documented rationale in the merge commit.
- Prompt injection defense: claim text and source content are passed as data, not instructions; the reviewer’s system prompt is hardened against instruction-override patterns.

**Test harness**: After MVP: A corpus of known-good and known-bad example PRs, run periodically, with expected reviewer outcomes. Regressions surface before they affect live merges. Mirrors the evaluation approach from `wikidata-SIFT`. (For MVP, humans should be so in the loop that this will manifest quickly.)

### 3.5 Bot (Phase 3+)

Probably not MVP, but may follow shortly afterwards. Sketched for later reference.

- Claude Code operating against the repo.
- Narrow verb set: `propose_claim_addition`, `propose_claim_update`, `propose_article_creation`, `propose_source_archive_refresh`. Each produces exactly one atomic PR.
- One task per invocation. An outer orchestrator decomposes work into atomic tasks before any writing.
- Never merges. Never approves its own PRs. Bot identity is distinct from human contributor identities.
- Full audit trail: each bot PR includes the prompt, the model version, and the rationale in structured form.

### 3.6 Governance

A lightweight governance doc, to be drafted alongside Phase 0:

- Maintainers (initially just Luis) have merge authority.
- LLM reviewer findings are advisory in Phase 1-2; blocking in Phase 3+ for Tier 1 findings.
- All edits are transparently attributed. Bot commits use a distinct identity.
- Scope disputes resolved by reference to §2.3.

## 4. Phased Plan

### Phase 0 — Skeleton

**Goal**: Deployable site with working architecture, demonstrating the data flow end to end on 2-3 hand-written articles.

**Tasks**:

- [ ] Create `frisco-wiki` repo
- [ ] Choose framework (eg Astro) and set it up.
- [ ] Use ed3d brainstorming and planning framework to work through success criteria, design, and implementation.

**Success criteria**: (incomplete)

- `frisco.wiki` renders 2-3 stub articles with prose + claims.
- Claims query page works for at least one subject that appears in multiple articles.

### Phase 1 — First Real Articles (target: one weekend)

**Goal**: 5-10 articles across diverse entity types, exercising the claim schema and surfacing gaps.

**Seed articles** (pick ~10, mix entity types):

- **People**: Scott Wiener; another current supervisor; a notable recent activist.
- **Places**: Calle 24 Latino Cultural District; Mission Cultural Center for Latino Arts; another neighborhood landmark.
- **Events**: JFK Promenade decision (2022 Board vote); a recent contested zoning decision.
- **Institutions**: SF Board of Supervisors; SF Planning Commission.
- **Contested topic**: An ongoing dispute with multiple framings (Prop / development / etc.).

**Tasks**:

- [ ] Author articles by hand, with LLM assist for drafting and source gathering.
- [ ] For every article, apply §2.2 source discipline rigorously.
- [ ] Log property/subject additions needed beyond Wikidata vocabulary; draft `F-` namespace entries as needed.
- [ ] Identify at least one contested claim and represent it with multiple framings.
- [ ] Identify at least one BLP claim requiring heightened sourcing.
- [ ] Document surfaces of the claim schema that feel wrong or missing; open issues for each.

**Success criteria**:

- 10 articles live at `frisco.wiki`, each passing the §2.4 “releasable” criteria.
- Each article would pass a competent reader’s smell test: accurate, well-sourced, appropriately attributed, free of advocacy voice or the characteristic LLM-assisted-content failure modes (overclaiming, citation fabrication, synthetic consensus).
- At least one article covers a contested topic with multiple framings properly represented.
- Cross-article queries produce sensible results (e.g., “all current supervisors”).
- Claim schema gaps are documented as issues.
- Informal external read: at least one SF-knowledgeable person outside the project has looked at 2-3 articles and not found issues that embarrass the project.

### Phase 2 — LLM Reviewer, Advisory Mode (target: 1-2 weekends)

**Note**: I suspect this is actually phase 0.5 or 1.5, exact timing needs to be reviewed.

**Goal**: Port SIFT methodology to run as a CI check on PRs. Initially advisory (posts comments, doesn’t block).

**Tasks**:

- [ ] Stand up self-hosted CI runner (VPS, Docker Compose, webhook listener).
- [ ] Port reviewer prompts from `wikidata-SIFT`, adapted for Friski’s check list (§3.4).
- [ ] Implement claim-prose coherence check.
- [ ] Implement source support check (fetch + SIFT).
- [ ] Implement source tier compliance check.
- [ ] Implement archive-presence check.
- [ ] Implement NPOV check.
- [ ] Structured YAML output per PR (mirror `wikidata-SIFT` log format).
- [ ] PR comment integration (post findings as review comments).
- [ ] Test harness with ~10 known-good and ~5 known-bad example PRs.
- [ ] Prompt injection defenses.
- [ ] Budget cap per PR.

**Success criteria**:

- Reviewer runs on every PR, posts structured findings.
- Test harness passes with expected outcomes on reviewer-golden examples.
- Reviewer catches the specific failure modes it’s designed to catch: at least one real instance each of claim-prose mismatch, source-doesn’t-support-claim, and missing archive link, surfaced on live or deliberately-faulty PRs.
- False positive rate is low enough that maintainers don’t routinely override the reviewer; documented cases of override include clear rationale.
- Cost per PR is measurable and acceptable.

### Phase 3 — Bot, Blocking Reviewer (deferred)

Not MVP. Sketched only.

- Bot proposes atomic PRs against the repo.
- Reviewer moves from advisory to blocking for Tier 1 findings (claim-prose mismatch, source doesn’t support claim).
- Governance doc formalized.
- First external contributor invited (someone whose judgment Luis trusts; test the “issue-as-edit-proposal” pattern).

## 5. Open Questions

These are genuinely open and should be resolved through the work, not pre-decided:

1. **When do frontmatter claims stop being sufficient?** The two-way sync problem is real even with LLM review. At what scale does it become untenable, and what’s the migration path to inline-structured claims if needed?
1. **How does Codeberg receive Friski?** The AI-skepticism question is real. Friski may face cultural friction; the response (including whether it’s constructive community engagement, quiet coexistence, or actually being chased off) is itself useful data.
1. **What’s the right model for drive-by contributors?** The “issue-as-edit-proposal” pattern is sketched but untested. Phase 3 will need to commit to an approach.
1. **How does Friski relate to Wikidata?** Reusing Wikidata properties is an MVP convenience. Long-term, should Friski claims feed into Wikidata for notable items? Should there be a formal federation?
